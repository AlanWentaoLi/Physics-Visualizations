Instructions for use

This is the guidance for the Green Theorem visualisation.

The graph shows the relationship between microscopic circulation and macroscopic circulation. From the graph you can see by adding up the microscopic circulations, the inner arrows will cancel with each other. Hence the sole effect of piling up the microscopic circulations will be the macroscopic circulation.

By changing the slider, more microscopic circulations will be added in the graph.

More details are shown here.

Have fun!

Green's Theorem

The arrows for microscopic circulation is colour-coded.
From the diagram, we can see that every arrows inside the grid is paired with an arrow with opposite direction. In other words, all the magenta arrows will cancel each other and we are left with green arrows
The relationship between macroscopic and microscopic circulation becomes apparent. After the cancellation, we are left with macroscopic circulation. This link is named Green's Theorem: \[ \oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot \, d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{\hat{k}} \, dS. \] where \(\mathbf{A}\) is the vector field defined for the area inside the closed curve, \(D\).
For \(\mathbf{A} = (P,Q,R) \), Green's Theorem is reduced to, \[ \oint_C \,P(x,y(x))\,dx + Q(x,x(y))\,dy = \iint_D \left[ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}(x,\,y) - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}(x,\,y) \right] \,dx\,dy \]
