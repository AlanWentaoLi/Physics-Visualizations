Circulation Integrals

Consider a unit square, \(D\), surrounded by a closed loop \(C\). The circulation \((\Gamma)\) of a vector field is defined as the line integral of the vector field \((\vec{A})\) around a closed loop C. In 2D, we let \(\vec{A}=(L,\,M)\) then: $$ \Gamma = \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot \, d\vec{r} = \oint_C L\,dx+M\,dy $$ This is a measure of how much the vector field is "spinning" overall. Remember the right hand rule!

Curl

Recall the definition of a curl of a vector field \(\vec{A}\) is: $$ \text{curl}\,\vec{A} = \nabla \times \vec{A} $$ We can think of the curl as a measure of "spinning per unit area". If we only consider vector fields in 2D (i.e. \(\vec{A} = (L,M,0) \)) we can "add up" all of these little rotating regions by integrating the curl flux through our surface (in this case the \(x-y\) plane) like: $$ \iint_D \text{curl}\,\vec{A} \cdot \vec{n} \, dS = \iint_D \Big( \frac{\partial M}{\partial x} -\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} \Big) \, dxdy $$ N.B. The curl of a 2D vector field has only one component in the \(\mathbf{\hat{k}}\) -direction. Try calculating it yourself!

Overall

Now we make the link between these 2 integrals. Consider breaking down our region \(D\) into an \(n\times n\) grid (strictly we would need to take \(n\to\infty\) but this would be impossible to see). Now we have tiny rotating regions of our curl. However, if we look inside our grid, every arrow has a corresponding opposite arrow. These arrows, coloured green, "cancel" out and the only ones left are the ones coloured magenta. Increase n using the slider and you will see this is always the case.

Finally, we notice that after all the green arrows "cancel", the magenta arrows make up the line integral we saw earlier. This is the link between the circulation integral and curl flux integral which gives us Green's Theorem: $$ \oint_C L\,dx+M\,dy = \iint \frac{\partial M}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial L}{\partial y}\,dx\,dy$$
