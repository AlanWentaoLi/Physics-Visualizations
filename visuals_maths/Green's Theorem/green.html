Circulation Integrals

Consider a unit square, \(D\), surrounded by a closed loop \(C\). The circulation \((\Gamma)\) of a vector field is defined as the line integral of the vector field \((\vec{A})\) around a closed loop C. In 2D, we let \(\vec{A}=(P,\,Q,\,0)\) then: $$ \Gamma = \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot \, d\vec{r} = \oint_C P(x,\,y(x))\,dx+Q(y,\,x(y))\,dy $$ N.B. When integrating \(P\) and \(Q\), remember to convert \(P\) as a function of \(x\) only and \(Q\) as a function of \(y\) only.

Circulation is a measure of how much the vector field is "spinning" overall or it is sometimes called the macroscopic circulation . Remember by convention we go around line integrals anti-clockwise, use the right hand rule to remember this!

Curl

Recall the definition of the curl of a vector field \(\vec{A}\) is: $$ \text{curl}\,\vec{A} = \nabla \times \vec{A} $$ We can think of the curl as a measure of "spinning per unit area". If we only consider vector fields in 2D (i.e. \(\vec{A} = (P,Q,0) \)) and we "add up" all of these little rotating regions by integrating the curl flux through our surface (in this case the \(x-y\) plane) like: $$ \iint_D (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot \mathbf{\hat{k}} \, dS = \iint_D \Big( \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} -\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \Big) \, dxdy $$ We can think of the curl flux integral as being all the infinitesimally small regions of rotations "added up". This is sometimes called microscopic circulation.

N.B. The curl of a 2D vector field has only one component in the \(\mathbf{\hat{k}}\) -direction. Try calculating it yourself.

Overall

Now, we make the link between these 2 integrals. Consider breaking down our region \(D\) into an \(n\times n\) grid.

Inside each small box, we have tiny rotating regions of the curl of our vector field. However, if we look inside our grid, every arrow has a corresponding opposite arrow. These arrows, coloured green, "cancel" out and the only ones left are the ones coloured magenta. Increase \(n\) using the slider and you will see this is always the case.

Finally, we notice that after all the green arrows "cancel", the magenta arrows make up the same contour as in the line integral we saw earlier. This is the link between macroscopic and microscopic circulation which gives us Green's Theorem: $$ \oint_C P(x,y(x))\,dx+Q(x,x(y))\,dy = \iint_D \Big(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}- \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\Big)\,dx\,dy$$
