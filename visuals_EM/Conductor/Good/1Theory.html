Good Conductor - Theory

For a Good Conductor \(\varepsilon \rightarrow \varepsilon_{0}\) and \(\omega\varepsilon_{0}/\sigma\lt\lt 1\). The dispersion relation of the conductor is given by $$k^{2} = \mu_{0}\varepsilon\omega^{2}(1 + \frac{i\sigma}{\varepsilon\omega}) \simeq i\mu_{0}\sigma\omega$$ The wave vector \(\mathbf{k}\) is complex, \(\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{k}_{R} +i\mathbf{k}_{I}\), thus $$ k^{2} = (\mathbf{k}_{R} +i\mathbf{k}_{I})\cdot(\mathbf{k}_{R} +i\mathbf{k}_{I}) = \mathbf{k}^{2}_{R} - \mathbf{k}^{2}_{I} + i2\mathbf{k}_{R}\cdot\mathbf{k}_{I}$$ $$Re(k^{2}) = 0 \rightarrow k_{R} = k_{I},\quad Im(k^{2}) = \mu_{0}\sigma\omega$$ and assuming the real and imaginary components are parallel then $$k_{R} = k_{I} = \frac{1}{\delta}\quad where\quad \delta = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\mu_{0}\sigma\omega}}$$ Therefore the phase velocity is greatly reduced as $$ v_{phase} = \sqrt{\frac{2\omega\varepsilon_{0}}{\sigma}}c \lt\lt c $$ moreover the wave is strongly attenuated as $$\lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k_{R}} = 2\pi\delta$$ with \(B_{0}\) lagging behind \(E_{0}\) by \(\pi/4\) as $$tan(\phi) = \frac{Z_{I}}{Z_{R}} = \frac{-k_{I}}{k_{R}} = -1$$ The wave propagates but is strongly attenuated over a distance \(\delta\).
