The Centrifugal Force (given by \(-m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}\))) is one of the three inertial forces that arise from a rotating frame. This force always points out away from the Earth and so acts against gravity. In fact it acts as a small correction to it, changing both the magnitude and the direction.

The Earth's rotation distorts it to an oblate spheroid (like a squashed orange). This causes a plumb-line to appear as if it hangs vertically because the normal to the Earth's surface does not point exactly to the centre of the Earth. As a result, the centrifugal term does not lead to any deviations in the plane of the Earth's surface. Therefore, the centrifugal term is negligible for many experiments on Earth.

However, it is important to know how it can have a meaningful impact. In our case, the rotation speed only leads to a difference in diameter of \(22km\) between the poles and the equator. How would increasing the speed affect the shape?
